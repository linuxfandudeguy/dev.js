My Epic Struggle with Electron: A Tale of Packaging Woes

It all began with my ambitious project to create a desktop application using Electron. My setup started on a ChromeOS machine, which is not traditionally known for being ideal for such tasks. But I dove in anyway, determined to make it work.

Initially, I set up my project directory and began the process of packaging my files. That’s when the real trouble started. I ran the command to package everything, only to find myself repeatedly modifying the package.json file in what felt like an endless cycle. The constant changes were maddening, and I installed an array of packages build-essential libgtk-3-dev libnss3 libxkbcommon-x11-dev most of which turned out to be unnecessary.

After struggling with a particular packager for over an hour, I discovered that it didn’t even work. Frustrated, I switched to a different packager, hoping for a better outcome. Finally, after significant effort and persistence, the successful packager managed to complete the process.

When it was all done, I delivered the packaged files to Google Drive for distribution. The moment of triumph was short-lived, however, as I was still stuck with the aftermath of my struggles.

To clean up, I executed the dreaded rm -rf command to delete the project directory. It felt like a cleansing act after the chaos I had endured. Despite the cleanup, I was relieved to see my computer running faster and more efficiently.

Looking back, I can’t help but agree with those who criticize Electron. The process was tortuous, and the excessive RAM usage was beyond frustrating. I spent countless hours dealing with issues, and the end result, while successful, came at a significant cost in terms of time and sanity.

In the end, I made a desktop application, but the experience has left me with a newfound respect for those who navigate Electron’s complexities.
